Part I

THE SITUATION

What you are looking at: a healthy customer base inside a cost structure built for a growth rate the company does not have.

Section 1

Executive Summary

Situation

RedTeam has a healthy customer base and a deep product. In the last 30 days, 7,878 active projects managed $10.3 billion in construction value through the platform.1 The customer economics are fundamentally sound: 528 of 544 customers (97%) have positive direct contribution margin, generating $4,873,430 at a 71% contribution margin. Only 16 customers are truly cost-negative. This is a construction vertical leader with real enterprise value in its existing base.

Complication

The cost structure is nearly 2x what the contribution can support. Company-level overhead totals $9,111,994 (P&E $4,132,028, S&M $3,185,960, G&A $1,794,006) against $4,873,430 in contribution — producing a -45% EBITDA margin. Three specific cost structure failures drive the gap:

  1. S&M burns $3,185,960/yr acquiring customers at negative ROI. In 2025, $3,185,960 in S&M produced $1,323,265 in new ARR — but churn exceeded new, and ARR declined by $160,530. True CAC is ~$33,502 per customer with a ~3.7-year payback. At 81% NRR, 57% of new customers churn before payback. This is the single largest waste in the P&L.
  2. The support burden is a product quality tax, not a customer problem. 81% of customers file zero tickets. The top 10% of customers generate 91% of all support volume. HubSpot ticket analysis (16,436 tickets, Jan 2024 – Mar 2026)2 shows 49% of tickets are system-generated. Of human tickets, product issues dominate: integration failures (Sage 300 CRE: 138, QBO: 148, Sage 100: 78), login assistance (478 tickets), and Flex legacy bugs (87% of all escalations). The support team is actually efficient — median resolution is 6 minutes, 62% close in under 1 hour. The “noisy customers” are power users hitting integration bugs, not bad customers.
  3. Pricing has no expansion mechanism. Over 90% of customers are on capped or fixed pricing. NRR structurally cannot exceed 100% without manual price increases. The pricing model makes growth investment unproductive — even a perfect product cannot drive expansion revenue on flat-rate pricing.

Resolution

The customer base is sound — the mature base (2+ years) retains at 94.3% Revenue GRR.3 The cost structure needs to match that reality. Four levers, in order of speed and impact, create a path from ($3,638,485) to +$1,362,547–$2,602,155 EBITDA:

  1. Cut S&M and rationalize CS (immediate, +$4,057,119 EBITDA impact). S&M at $3,185,960 spent $2.41 for every $1 of new ARR and produced a net ARR decline. CS is sized for 100+ new customers/year when economics justify 5-10. Combined savings: $4,057,119/year.
  2. Fix integration quality, then monetize (Days 30-60). Sage 300 CRE (138 tickets) and QBO (148 tickets) are both churn drivers and upsell opportunities. Stabilize first, then charge $1,200-$2,400/year. Integration and product bundling are the NRR engines the flat-rate pricing model has been missing — $798,285-$1,310,991 in incremental ARR from existing customers alone.
  3. Establish price floors and move to project-based pricing (Days 60-100). $15K minimum + $5K per project beyond 3. The GC passes the project fee to the developer as a Division 01 line item — 0.35% of project cost. 438 customers move to the $15K floor (+$3,097,041 day-one). Project fees at 328 new projects/month add $11,535,000/yr. Total revenue impact: +$14,632,041 (+213%).
  4. Optional: P&E rationalization (30% cut = +$1,239,608). Depends on platform migration timeline. Not required for breakeven.

The Math Is Broken

At a 1.5x LTV:CAC, RedTeam loses money on every new customer acquired. CAC payback is 29 months, but annual ARR churn is 20% — meaning the average customer churns before they even pay back acquisition costs.

This means growth is not the answer. Adding more customers at these economics just accelerates the burn. The path to profitability runs through: (1) improving retention (reduce churn from 20% to <10%), (2) increasing ARPU to shorten payback, and (3) reducing S&M spend until unit economics are fixed.

Benchmark: Procore's CAC payback was 19 months at IPO — but their NRR was 117-125%, meaning expansion revenue made up for the long payback. RedTeam's NRR of 81% means there is no expansion engine to compensate.

The S&M Spend Produced Negative Growth at a Cost of $3,185,960

In 2025, RedTeam added $1,323,265 in new ARR but lost $1,483,795 to churn — a net ARR decline of $-160,530. The company spent $3,185,960 on Sales & Marketing to produce that $1,323,265 — a 2.4x cost ratio. At a true CAC of ~$33,502 per customer and a ~4-year payback, 57% of new customers will churn before they ever return a positive dollar. Approximately $1,807,724 of the S&M budget was spent on customers who will never pay back.

Cutting S&M is the single fastest path to breakeven. Had S&M been cut to zero in 2025, the company would have lost the same $1,483,795 to churn but saved $3,185,960 in spend — a $3,346,490 improvement to EBITDA.

Price floors fix the bottom. But the structural problem is that RedTeam charges a flat subscription for a project-based industry. Construction companies don’t have “recurring” workloads — they have projects. Every project has a start, a budget, a team, and an end. The pricing should match.

Why This Has Always Been Obvious — And Overlooked

Every general contractor already has a technology cost code in their Division 01 General Conditions. It is CSI MasterFormat code 01-750 (Field Engineering / Technology) or similar — right next to temporary power (01-510), dumpster service (01-740), and safety equipment (01-560). It is a standard, expected project cost that appears on every AIA G702/G703 pay application. The developer/owner sees it on Draw 1 alongside every other general conditions item and approves it without a second thought.

The cost code already exists. The billing mechanism already exists. The developer already expects to pay for project infrastructure. The only thing missing is RedTeam putting itself on the line item.

This is a one-time setup. The GC adds “Construction Management Technology Platform” to their standard schedule of values template. From that point forward, every new project automatically includes it. There is no per-project negotiation, no approval process, no sales conversation with the owner. It is a general conditions cost — the same way nobody negotiates the dumpster rental line by line.

Every construction SaaS company in the market charges the GC a subscription and hopes the GC absorbs it as overhead. That is why construction software has high churn, poor NRR, and low willingness-to-pay. The entire industry has been billing the wrong party. The developer is the one who benefits from the technology — their project runs on time, on budget, with full documentation. They should pay for it. And they will, because it is already a line item in the cost structure of every project in America. It just says “$0” right now.

The Developer Gets Billed — Not the GC

The $5,000 per-project fee is a project cost billed to the developer/owner on the AIA pay application, Draw 1. It is not a GC expense. It is not optional. It is how construction billing works — the GC includes project costs on the schedule of values, the developer approves the draw, and the money flows. This is identical to how the GC bills for the crane, the dumpster, the temporary fencing, and the porta-johns. Nobody asks the GC to eat those costs. Nobody will ask them to eat this one.

RedTeam generates the compliant billing documentation. When a project is created, the platform produces a project technology invoice that maps directly to the GC’s schedule of values — pre-formatted for AIA G703 continuation sheets. The GC includes it on the next draw. No manual invoicing. No awkward conversations with the owner. One line item, one time, done.

The GC Makes Money — Two Ways

1. Markup on the technology fee. The GC passes through the $5,000 and adds a $2,000+ orchestration/coordination fee for managing the platform, onboarding subcontractors, administering users, and running the technology side of the project. This is standard practice — same as marking up equipment rental. On a $2M project with 65% construction financing at 8%, the developer pays ~$285/day in interest alone. The total $7,000 technology fee is less than 25 days of interest — 0.35% of total project cost. Nobody blinks.

2. Annual technology administration fee. For multi-year projects, the GC can bill an annual technology administration and maintenance fee (e.g. $1,500-$2,500/year) to cover ongoing software management, user coordination, and reporting. This is recurring revenue for the GC — funded by the developer, not by RedTeam.

Combined: the GC pays RedTeam $35,000/year, bills developers $49,000+ (plus annual admin fees on multi-year projects), and nets +$14,000+. The software pays for itself and then some.
