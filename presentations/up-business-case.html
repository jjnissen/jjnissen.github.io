Industry Overview

A $2.17 trillion market that still runs on paper, spreadsheets, and text messages.

Market Size and Structure

The U.S. construction industry generated $2.17 trillion in total spending in 2025, making it one of the largest sectors of the American economy. This figure encompasses residential, commercial, industrial, and infrastructure projects across every state. The industry is composed of approximately 920,000 construction establishments employing 8.31 million workers, making it the nation's largest employer of field-based labor. Despite its scale, the industry is remarkably fragmented: the vast majority of firms employ fewer than 20 people, and even the largest general contractors rarely control more than 1–2% of their local markets.

The Digitization Gap

Construction is the second-least digitized major sector in the global economy, trailing only mining and agriculture, according to McKinsey's industry digitization index. While manufacturing productivity has grown roughly 5x over the past 20 years, construction labor productivity grew at just 0.4% per year over the same period—essentially flat. This productivity stagnation is directly attributable to the industry's resistance to technology adoption and the absence of integrated software tools suited to how construction companies actually operate.

The consequences of this gap are measured in the hundreds of billions. An estimated $177 billion is lost annually in the United States alone due to manual process inefficiency—data entered multiple times across disconnected systems, miscommunication between field and office, rework caused by outdated drawings, and payment delays driven by paperwork bottlenecks.

The ConTech Market

Construction technology (ConTech) is a recognized and growing category. The market was valued at approximately $7 billion in 2025 and is projected to reach $30 billion by 2034, representing a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of roughly 17%. This growth is being driven by three converging forces: (1) generational turnover bringing digitally native operators into leadership, (2) labor shortages making efficiency gains non-optional, and (3) the maturation of AI/ML tools capable of processing the unstructured data (photos, drawings, emails, texts) that define construction workflows.

Section 03

How Construction Projects Work

A primer for anyone unfamiliar with the industry's workflows, stakeholders, and financial structures.

The Phases of a Construction Project

Every construction project, from a $500K tenant improvement (a buildout of leased space) to a $500M hospital, follows a similar lifecycle composed of four major phases:

  1. Pre-Construction: The project is conceived, designed, estimated, and bid. This is where architects produce drawings, engineers design the structural and mechanical systems, estimators calculate costs, and the owner selects a general contractor (GC). This phase can last 3–18 months.
  2. Procurement: The GC hires subcontractors (subs), orders materials, secures permits, and establishes the project schedule. Subcontractor agreements, purchase orders, and insurance certificates are generated and exchanged. A $20M project may involve 40–60 separate subcontractor agreements.
  3. Construction: Physical building occurs. The superintendent manages the jobsite day to day. The project manager tracks budget, schedule, submittals (documents showing what will be installed before it's installed), RFIs (Requests for Information—formal questions to the architect when drawings are unclear), change orders (modifications to the original scope and price), and pay applications (monthly invoices from subs to the GC, and from the GC to the owner).
  4. Closeout: The project is completed, inspected, and handed over. This involves punch lists (lists of incomplete items), as-built drawings, warranties, operations manuals, and final payment. Closeout is consistently the most neglected phase and the one where money is most frequently left on the table.

The Documents

Construction generates an enormous volume of documents. A typical $10M commercial project produces 3,000–5,000 individual documents over its lifecycle. The critical document types include:

  • Drawings and Specifications: The architectural and engineering plans that define what gets built. Hundreds of pages.
  • Submittals: Product data sheets, shop drawings, and samples sent by subs to the architect for approval before installation.
  • RFIs (Requests for Information): Formal written questions when the drawings are unclear or conflicting.
  • Change Orders: Modifications to the original contract scope and price. The average commercial project has 35–50 change orders.
  • Pay Applications: Monthly billing documents (typically using AIA G702/G703 forms) showing work completed and money owed. These flow from sub to GC to owner.
  • Lien Waivers: Legal documents where a party waives their right to place a lien (a legal claim) on the property in exchange for payment. Required before every payment.
  • Daily Logs: Records of weather, manpower, equipment, work performed, and safety incidents. Created every day on every project.

How Money Flows

Construction has a unique financial structure that differs from nearly every other industry:

The Payment Chain Developer/Owner → General Contractor → Subcontractors → Suppliers

Money flows down the chain through progress billing: each month, subs submit pay applications to the GC showing work completed; the GC bundles them, adds its own charges, and submits to the owner. The owner pays the GC, who then pays the subs. This cycle typically takes 45–90 days from work performed to payment received.

Retention (also called retainage) adds another layer: the owner withholds 5–10% of every payment as security until the project is complete. On a $20M project, that's $1–2M sitting in retention.

Job Costing, WIP, and the Surety Relationship

Job costing is the accounting method where every expense and revenue item is tracked against a specific project. Unlike a retail business that tracks costs by product, a construction company tracks costs by job. This means every labor hour, material purchase, equipment rental, and subcontractor payment is coded to a specific project and cost code.

The WIP schedule (Work in Progress) is the single most important financial document a GC produces. It compares the percentage of work completed on each project against the percentage of contract value billed. If you've completed 60% of the work but only billed 50%, you're "underbilled"—meaning you've invested more cash than you've collected. The opposite is "overbilling." Both have major implications for financial reporting and cash flow management.

The surety relationship is critical and often misunderstood. Before a GC can bid most projects over $500K, they need a performance bond—essentially insurance that guarantees the GC will complete the work. The surety company (think: CNA, Travelers, Liberty Mutual) underwrites the GC based on their financial statements, WIP schedule, and track record. The surety reviews the GC's financials annually and sets a bonding capacity—the maximum total value of bonded projects the GC can carry at once. This is why the WIP schedule and accurate job costing are existential requirements, not nice-to-haves: lose your bonding and you cannot bid work.

Why This Matters for UP The surety/WIP requirement is both the biggest barrier to switching accounting systems and UP's biggest opportunity. GC accountants won't switch off QuickBooks or Sage because their surety CPAs need historical WIP data. UP doesn't ask them to switch. UP writes journal entries directly into their existing accounting software. The accountant keeps their tools and their surety relationship. UP adds intelligence on top.

Tool Sprawl and Data Silos

The average general contractor uses 6.2 separate software tools to run their business, according to JBKnowledge's annual construction technology survey. A typical mid-market GC ($10M–$100M revenue) has separate systems for project management (maybe Procore, maybe spreadsheets), estimating (a desktop tool or Excel), accounting (QuickBooks for Construction or Sage 300), time tracking (a mobile app or paper timesheets), document management (Dropbox, Google Drive, or a filing cabinet), and scheduling (Primavera, MS Project, or a whiteboard). None of these systems talk to each other natively. Data entered in one system must be manually re-entered in others.

The Accountant Problem

The construction accountant is the single most powerful technology gatekeeper in the company. They typically run QuickBooks for Construction (Intuit) or Sage 300 CRE, and they will not switch—not because the software is good, but because their surety's CPA firm needs historical WIP data in a format they recognize. The WIP schedule is the lifeline that maintains bonding capacity. Ask an accountant to switch accounting systems and they'll tell you: "You're asking me to risk our bonding." And they're right.

Manual Data Entry Everywhere

The same information is entered into multiple systems throughout a project's lifecycle:

  • A subcontractor's bid amount is entered into the estimate, then re-entered into the contract, then re-entered into the budget, then re-entered into the accounting system.
  • A change order starts as a field conversation, becomes a text message, then an email, then a spreadsheet, then a formal CO document, then a budget revision, then a pay app line item.
  • Daily logs: the superintendent writes notes on paper or into an app, which the PM then summarizes, which the office then enters into the PM system.
  • Time tracking: workers fill out paper timesheets or a mobile app; the PM reviews them; the accountant re-enters into payroll.

Communication Chaos

Perhaps the most destructive problem: construction communication happens in text messages, phone calls, and emails—none of which are captured, searchable, or linked to a project record. A superintendent texts a sub about a scope change. That text is a binding conversation with financial implications, but it exists only on two personal phones. When a dispute arises six months later, nobody can find it. The industry's institutional memory is scattered across thousands of iMessage threads and voicemails that nobody saved.

The Core Problem Statement Construction companies don't need more software. They need less software that does more. They need a single system where communication IS the data entry, where every conversation is searchable and auditable, and where the accountant doesn't have to change anything.

Four standalone profit centers, each with its own KPIs, revenue model, and compounding dynamics.

Not Day 1. Year 1+.

Float 2: Regulatory Capture

8 proprietary databases. $33–82M estimated replacement cost.

UP maintains eight proprietary databases, each curated and updated continuously by autonomous AI steward agents (specialized AI programs that run 24/7, monitoring government websites, regulatory filings, and public data sources to keep each database current without human intervention):

  1. Tax Database: Federal, state, and local tax rules across all 50 states, including prevailing wage rates (government-mandated minimum wages for each trade on public projects) and union requirements.
  2. Sales Tax Database: Sales tax rates and exemptions across 12,000+ local jurisdictions. Construction materials have different tax treatment in almost every state.
  3. Permits Database: Requirements, fee schedules, and filing processes for 19,000+ building departments nationwide.
  4. Cost Data: Real-time material prices pulled from FRED (Federal Reserve Economic Data), BLS (Bureau of Labor Statistics), and major distributors like Home Depot and Ferguson.
  5. CSI MasterFormat: The industry-standard classification system that organizes all construction work into numbered divisions (Division 03 = Concrete, Division 26 = Electrical, etc.).
  6. AiM (AI Models): Vision model training data—construction document examples, jobsite photos, and labeled datasets used to train UP's document intelligence.
  7. Ontology: The relationship graph that maps how all construction concepts connect to each other.
  8. Skills: A marketplace of learned patterns—workflows, prompts, and automations that crystallize from usage and can be shared between companies.

For context, Gordian/RSMeans—the industry standard for construction cost data alone—charges $999–$60,000/year for access to their data. UP's eight databases collectively represent an estimated $33–82M in replacement cost and become more valuable every day whether or not anyone uses the platform.

Section 08

Technology Infrastructure

A graph database, a hash-chained ledger, and an AI agent runtime.

The Ontology Graph

At the core of UP is a knowledge graph built on just 4 PostgreSQL tables: nodes, edges, events, and action_types. Nodes represent entities (people, companies, projects, documents, cost codes). Edges represent relationships between them. Events represent actions taken. Action_types define the vocabulary of what can happen. This simplicity is intentional—the graph can represent any construction workflow without schema changes.

Hash-Chained Event Ledger

Every action in UP is recorded as an event in the ledger. Each event includes a SHA-256 hash (a cryptographic fingerprint) that incorporates the hash of the previous event, creating an unbroken chain. This means no event can be altered or deleted without breaking the chain—providing an immutable, auditable record of every action taken on every project. This is not blockchain (there is no distributed consensus, no cryptocurrency, no gas fees). It is a practical application of cryptographic chaining to create trust in project records.

Triple-Entry Accounting

The hash-chained ledger creates what accountants call "triple-entry accounting" as a natural byproduct. Traditional double-entry accounting records debits and credits. Triple-entry adds a third record—a cryptographically verified receipt that both parties can independently verify. When a subcontractor submits a pay application and the GC approves it, the ledger records both sides of the transaction plus a shared, tamper-proof receipt. This eliminates disputes over what was agreed.

The Agent Runtime

UP's AI capabilities are powered by an agent runtime built on Claude's tool-use architecture with a Karpathy 3-register system (a prompting methodology where each AI agent maintains three distinct context registers: system knowledge, task instructions, and conversation history). Agents use 15 shared MCP tools (Model Context Protocol—standardized interfaces that allow AI models to interact with databases, APIs, and external systems) to read from and write to the graph.

Grand Central Event Bus

All system events flow through a central event bus built on PostgreSQL's native LISTEN/NOTIFY capability, enabling parallel delivery to any number of subscribers. When a change order is approved, the event bus simultaneously notifies the budget agent (update the forecast), the accounting agent (create a journal entry), the PM (alert on Slack or in-app), and the sub (your CO was approved). This architecture eliminates polling, reduces latency, and ensures nothing falls through the cracks.

Skills: Learned and Shared Patterns

As users interact with UP, the system identifies repeating patterns and crystallizes them into Skills—reusable automations that can be forked (copied and customized) and shared between companies. A GC in Phoenix who develops a workflow for Arizona ROC licensing can share that Skill with every GC in Arizona. Skills compound across the user base, making the platform smarter for everyone.

Section 17

Defensibility / Moats

Four compounding moats. Bloomberg has two. Tesla has two. UP targets all four.

Moat 1: Data

UP's 8 proprietary databases represent an estimated $33–82M in replacement cost. But the real defensibility isn't the cost to build—it's the time. These databases are maintained by autonomous steward agents running 24/7, continuously scraping, parsing, and updating data from 50 state tax authorities, 12,000+ sales tax jurisdictions, 19,000+ building departments, and dozens of federal data sources. A competitor starting today would need 12–24 months of continuous data collection to reach parity, and by then UP's databases would be another 12–24 months deeper.

Moat 2: Network

Every project creates a temporary network of 15–60 companies. Every project on UP is a distribution event. The K-factor exceeds 1.0, meaning the network grows faster than linearly. Construction is an industry built on relationships and repeat business—the same GCs work with the same subs on project after project. Once a GC's sub network is on UP, switching costs become prohibitive. You're not switching a tool; you're switching a communication network.

Moat 3: Learning

Every user correction trains the model. Every document classified improves classification accuracy. Every Skills pattern shared makes the platform smarter for everyone. This is the Tesla Autopilot model applied to construction intelligence: more users = more data = better models = more users. The learning compounds exponentially, and no competitor can replicate the volume of labeled construction data without the user base to generate it.

Moat 4: Switching

UP's hash-chained audit trail, project history, team communication history, and learned Skills create formidable switching costs. Leaving UP means abandoning:

  • Cryptographically verified project records (useful for legal disputes, audits, and surety reviews)
  • Years of searchable communication history
  • Trained AI models customized to your company's patterns
  • Your entire sub network's presence on the platform
  • Skills and automations built over time

Triple Compounding

The Compounding Effect Most technology companies achieve one or two compounding effects. Bloomberg has data + switching costs but limited network effects. Tesla has data + learning but limited network effects. UP targets all three simultaneously: data + network + learning. Each reinforces the others. More users create more data, which improves the learning, which attracts more users, which generates more data. The flywheel accelerates over time.

Section 18

Risk Analysis

Six material risks, each with a specific mitigation strategy.
Construction Buyers Hate Technology
High Probability

The industry has a well-documented history of software adoption failure. 70% of GCs have no formal tech roadmap. Superintendents are skeptical of anything that isn't a text message.

Mitigation: UP IS a text message. The chat-first interface meets users where they already are. "Free for GCs" eliminates the budget objection. The product doesn't look or feel like enterprise software—it looks like iMessage with superpowers.
Procore Adds AI and Goes Down-Market
Medium Probability

Procore has $400M/year in R&D budget and has publicly committed to AI features. If they build a compelling AI layer and drop pricing to $15K, they could compete directly.

Mitigation: Large public companies can't easily canibalize their own ACV. Procore's sales org is built for $75K deals. Restructuring for $15K deals would require firing their enterprise team. Innovator's dilemma in action. Also: bolt-on AI is fundamentally different from AI-native architecture.
Embedded Banking is 12+ Months Out
Medium Impact

Float 1 (embedded finance) is a major long-term revenue driver but requires BaaS integration, compliance infrastructure, and regulatory approvals that take 12–18 months.

Mitigation: Float 1 revenue is excluded from Year 1 projections. The business is cash flow positive on software subscriptions alone. Embedded finance is upside, not a dependency.
Long Sales Cycles
High Probability

Construction sales cycles are typically 90–180 days. GC owners are cautious, relationship-driven, and suspicious of cold outreach.

Mitigation: PLG motion bypasses the sales cycle entirely. Individual $200/mo seats require no approval. Viral adoption through project invitations means subs join without a sales conversation. The direct sales pipeline is one channel, not the only channel.
Surety/WIP Integration Complexity
Medium Probability

Every GC's surety relationship depends on accurate WIP schedules generated from their accounting system. Any integration that disrupts WIP reporting risks the GC's bonding capacity.

Mitigation: UP does not replace the accounting system. It writes journal entries into the existing system (QBO, Sage). The accountant keeps their workflow. The surety sees no change. This is the key architectural decision that unlocks adoption.
LLM Cost Volatility
Low Probability

AI model costs could increase if providers change pricing, or usage per customer could exceed projections.

Mitigation: 2.3x headroom at current margins. LLM costs have declined 3x in 18 months and the trend is accelerating. Model routing (using cheaper models for simple tasks) and response caching further reduce costs. UP has architectural optionality to switch providers.
